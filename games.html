About the Project

Rope Tool, originally titled "Gmod Rope Tool: In Space" as a reference to Garry's Mod, was developed in Unity for the 2021 Ludum Dare. In order to fit with the Jam’s theme: “Unstable”, I proposed a physics based game where the player would have to figure out how to fly a spaceship around an area when all of its parts are only loosely tethered to the main cockpit. The idea was that the parts should be easy come, easy go. You may lose a thruster or two while locked in a heated space battle, but another one you can pick up shouldn’t be too far away.


I decided I liked the initial idea enough to keep the project going, and spent most of my summer refining the prototype. Currently my plan is to take the project through the IGM Production Studio class to continue its development.

Production Timeline

October 2021 - The project started as a submission for the Ludum Dare. In order to fit with the Jam’s theme: “Unstable”, I proposed a 2D physics based game where the player would control a spaceship that was perpetually falling apart. To be more specific, any thrusters and guns you collected became loosely tethered to your hull and would easily be knocked away in combat. With no good method of getting around without rockets, the final product ended up being very difficult to play, but it succeeded at being a fun experiment.


Summer 2022 - Over the summer I decided that I wanted to revisit the prototype we had made for the jam. It was obvious that the first thing it needed was a method of getting around that wouldn’t overshadow rocket collecting. I went with a grappling hook because I believed swinging could be used in tandem with rocket thrust to create some really satisfying movement. The majority of my time from that point forward was spent implementing this idea and refining the game’s mechanics to work around it.


Spring 2023 - I pitched my prototype to RIT’s Production Studio class with the goal of creating a vertical slice of a potential indie game. I recruited three of my classmates to work on narrative/level design, AI programming, and UI design respectively. I also found external team members for music, sound effects, and video production. In addition to directing the team, I created all the artwork/vfx and worked to refine the player controller.

My biggest challenge this semester was finding the most intuitive control scheme for the player movement. When you have several rockets attached to your ship, the player needs to be able to decide which ones they want active at a given time. By the end of the semester we settled on a rotating arrow; any rockets facing the same direction as the arrow will turn on when you hit the gas, which will move your ship in the direction the arrow is pointing.

Our final product included a dialogue-based tutorial, a “break the targets” level, and a boss fight. We brought what we made to three major events: the IGM Student Showcase, ImagineRIT, and RPI’s GameFest. For the RPI’s event we were selected from a pool of applicants and received a nomination for the Experimental Game Award.


Summer 2023 - I was fortunate to learn that the team wanted to continue working on the project beyond the scope of our class. Production continued steadily as we prepared to showcase a new build at the ROC Game Fest in September. Our efforts primarily focused on addressing the mountain of player feedback we had received from all of our playtest events. For me this primarily included bug fixes on the movement controller. Our biggest change from the previous build was the creation of a more beginner friendly boss. We also had several discussions about the future of the project and onboarded two new team members.

About the Project

Caravan of Cards was a semester-long project created for Tabletop RPG Design, a graduate level Game Design course. I and a team of four other students worked to develop a theme, style, world, and mechanics for an original tabletop RPG game. This culminated in a nearly 80+ page resource book for both players and game masters.

My Contributions

My major contribution to the book was the artwork featured as well as the powers/abilities section of character creation. The artwork I created includes the cover art, depictions of the world, and the 22 Major Arcana Tarot cards. My partner Lily Jenkins assisted me with design concepts for several of these cards and together we were able to complete the entire set within the rather small time frame of book creation. Because our game has a Tarot-based power system, each of the card artworks show depicts one of the abilities you will eventually unlock with that card. For example, the artwork for The Empress depicts a young woman painting flowers across the screen with a green brush stroke. This is meant to represent her card’s ability to summon plant life through artwork.

About the Project

Sunken Ship: Far From Home is an underwater exploration/shooter game that is being developed for a IGM Production Studio, a graduate-level class solely devoted to developing either new or in-progress projects over the course of a semester through mentorship, playtests, and agile development techniques.

My Contributions

My main contribution throughout this project has been the controls and movement of the player's submarine. We were very committed to evoking the feeling of being underwater with our gameplay. The game's movement was the subject of most of our in-class playtests and thus has gone through multiple stages of development. The initial version was a rover with a boost jump but was later changed to full three axis movement. I also programmed the responsive turbine animations.
